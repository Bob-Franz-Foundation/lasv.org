Welcome to our Logo page.  Here, you will learn how you can program using Logo.  Logo is a programming language that uses Input from the user, you, to create basic shapes, such as a square or a triangle, all the way to the advance stuff, making spheres and other 3d images. Whether you want to learn for fun or if you plan to take this seriously, these lessons will be fun and easy to learn.

 

        Logo syntax requires proper spacing.  Some of the errors that you will generate will often come from improper spacing. No spacing or to much spacing in between commands and there arguments (explained momentarily) will result in error messages.

 

        Consider this example:

                A. Yo uwil lallg oma dinfiv emi nu tes, ar eyou san ealre ady

 

        This is obviously very difficult to read without proper spacing.  Now, consider this:

B. You will all go mad in five minutes, are you sane already

 

        Better, wouldn’t you say?  It’s the same thing, only with good spacing. The computer is like a human.  If you don’t even know what you’re putting, neither will the computer.

 

        Home position is where your turtle (the little triangle if you are using MSWLogo or UCB Logo) starts at. It is the default position set by the creator to be the exact center of the page.  Whenever you clear screen (or cs, explained later), it will clear the whole picture you have made and return the turtle to its home position.

       

        The turtle is the little triangle in the middle of the page when you start up logo.  The turtle tells you one thing, the direction where your forwards, backwards, and other related inputs will go.  To change the direction of your input, you need to do right turns and left turns (rt and lt). The head of the turtle is the tip part of the triangle, ^.  The tail of the turtle would be the flat base of the triangle.

 

        The basic commands of Logo are the fd, the bk, the rt, and the lt.  Each particular primitive, which is what the commands of logo are called, requires an argument.  The arguments for these primitives are numbers.  For turns, the number represents the degree of turn for the turtle. For example, an rt 90 would result in the turtle changing its direction in a 90 degree angle. For forwards and backs, the number represents the distance your turtle will travel in the direction it is pointing.

 

        You could say that your turtle is lazy. Why you ask? Well, it hates to walk.  It has to do things that will make it easy for itself. So it uses a C.A.R.  The C.A.R. your turtle drives is like no other.  It drives a Command, Argument, and Return car.  Let’s take a look at this for a second

 

        For Example: The Command: fd, or forward, whichever you like; The Argument: 90, or any other number, but for this example we will use 90; And a Return, or enter.

       Hey, doesn't that look familiar?  Its the same box, with 7 less enters! A nice little shortcut, isn't it?  But there’s is a problem with it. Because you have no idea what you have drawn until you press enter, you have no idea until its too late if you have made an error or not! One important rule of this is to make sure you know what you are doing. If you are drawing a square, make sure that you have the proper turning degrees, the same degree of forwards, and the right number of arguments. With multiple line entries, as they are called, you can see each step, one by one, and easily see where your errors, if any, are at.
