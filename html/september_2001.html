WE FINISHED

By: Michael Marquez

I remember when we first proposed competing in the first American Solar race. We needed to build another chassis and body for our existing array. Solar Shadow II was to be a major improvement, including a race inspired suspension, a wheel motor, and Bridgestone tires. This car was to be grand, it would weigh less than the previous car. The only set back was that we had less than a year to finish and test the car.

We worked hard, by winter we had a rolling chassis, by June we were testing Solar Shadow. Tests were conducted at Salton Sea and on old Route 66. These trips gave us valuable information such as, array output, tire wear, and average watts per mile.

On July 5 the team left for Mighigan. Three days later we were working on the car in the parking structure at Western Michigan University The college teams were supportive, they gave us parts and technical support, which helped us pass scrutineering in one day.

On the fifth day we competed in the 250 mile qualifier at Gingerman Raceway. We ran well and qualified 24 out of 29 cars. This was the first time Solor Shadow ran without any real problems. After the qualifier we left for Chicago the official starting point of the American Solar Challenge. We spent the night in another KOA campground.

The next morning we awoke at 3AM and drove to the Chicago Museum of Science and Industry. Once there we did some last minute work on the car and lined up in our starting position. We waited anxiously for the start of the race. We said good luck to the other teams, met our first observer, and we ran for the vans as the flag dropped. The car was running, the race had began and it was a big relief for all of us. The traffic in down town was horrible. We ran into our first failures before we even got out of the city. A power tracker died and we didn't have any power coming from the array. We stopped and fixed it, we stopped and fixed it three times in all. At the end of day on we limped into Lincoln Land college. The next day there was no sun and we could not charge. We decided to trailer to the next check point, Missouri Rolla University, the truck pulling the trailer died. We found a GM dealership where we had it fixed. We were stuck for half the day, but the sun was shinning and we were able to fill our batteries.

Our next major problem was climbing 7200 ft. to Flagstaff. Solar Shadow made the climb with unexpected ease. On the long downhill out of Flagstaff, we were able to charge our batteries. Now all that was left was a trip through the California desert. The next couple of days seemed to just fly by. We were at a point now where we could run the car all day without any problems. In fact the Shadow ran so well that we would travel about a hundred miles extra every day.

Finally we arrived at Barstow, the final staging point. We met teams we hadn't seen since Chicago. We charged and got ready for the last day, everyone was tired but happy.


 Day 11, we had a three hour lead on the team behind us and we were two hours behind the car in front. We started well, a little way out of town our brakes over heated and began to rub. After stopping three times to fix them, we ran smoothly all the way to the finish line. We placed twenty second over all. In Claremont we felt satisfied that the car we built finished the first American Solar Challenge.
