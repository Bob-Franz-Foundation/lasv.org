National Electrathon

by: Joyce Chen

At the end of April, the Los Altos Solar Vehicle team attended the National Electrathon at the Encino velodrome. The team had rebuilt a body out of the original mold for Light Speed but this time, the vehicle would be without a solar array and with a canopy on top. After a difficult first day, consisting of electrical problems, the team was able to get the car running smoothly. Even with the problems faced by the team, they were able to pull together and finish sixth over all.

Human Powered Airplane

by: Jenny Liu

Since the summer of 1997, the Los Altos Academy of Engineering has been working to make a human powered airplane. IN August, they assembled the frame of the plane in the gymnasium at school and hopefully by November, they can get the insurance they need to be able to test the plane. Last year, they were unable to get properly insured and they don't want this to happen again. Although the amount of work put into this project was minimal during the school year, they are working hard this summer with the help of many talented people who have put together similar airplanes. Their plane, the Grasshopper, stands 23 ft high, 36 ft long, and has a 96 ft wingspan. Once this project is over, they hope to add it to their list of many achievements.

The new project for the solar vehicle team is to build Light Speed II. This vehicle will be a revised version of Light Speed. The team wants to be able to build a longer version of Light Speed in order to obtain more surface area to increase the number of solar cells on the car. They hope to enter this vehicle in the Winston Solar Challenge 2000 in Texas. More will be said about this project as it develops.

  Siemens Foundation
       Competition

by: James Lu

The Siemens Foundations is holding a national competition for high school science researchers this September. Here at the Los Altos Engineering Lab, Jason Zheng, Will Zheng, and James Lu are racing against time to try to finish their research for the competition. They are researching a way to quickly and fully charge a battery pack using cheap hardware. They've built the entire hardware part using B-Soft's I/O and relay boards. Although there are still some problems waiting to be solved, they are moving fast towards finishing the research. The team of three is also getting help from So. Cal Edison's Electric Vehicle department and AC Propulsion.

       The Sunburner

by: Joyce Chen

This year the academy started a new project, the Los Altos Solar Bike team. Headed by Jeff Lamb, math teacher, and Mark Gibson, physics teacher, the team built their first class-A solar bike. With a team of eight, they joined the solar vehicle team in Kansas for their first attempt at solar bike racing. They had three riders, Jeffery Hsiung, Charles Kim, and Timothy Chan. With a well built bike, the Sunburner, and a good team, they were able to finish thirteenth place on their very first try at this type of race. They finished in 3:07:17 with an average speed of 19.98 mph.
